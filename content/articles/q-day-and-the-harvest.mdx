The concept of "Q-Day" — the moment a sufficiently powerful quantum computer can break current public-key encryption standards — is no longer a distant theoretical concern. Intelligence agencies estimate this capability could emerge within the next decade, and some assessments place it sooner.

## The Harvest-Now, Decrypt-Later Threat

What makes Q-Day uniquely dangerous is that the threat operates retroactively. Nation-state actors are engaged in systematic collection of encrypted communications and data transfers today, storing them for future decryption once quantum capabilities mature.

This means that sensitive government communications, diplomatic cables, defense procurement data, and critical infrastructure control signals transmitted today are already compromised in a post-quantum future — unless they are protected by quantum-resistant cryptographic methods.

## Canada's Quantum Advantage

Canada possesses a natural advantage in the post-quantum transition. The country's quantum research ecosystem — anchored by institutions in Waterloo, Sherbrooke, and Vancouver — has produced foundational work in both quantum computing and quantum-safe cryptography.

This advantage, however, remains largely academic. The gap between research capability and operational deployment is where Canada must focus its efforts.
