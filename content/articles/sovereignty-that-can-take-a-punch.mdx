Sovereignty, in the digital age, is not a declaration. It is a capability. And capabilities, unlike declarations, must be tested under stress.

Prime Minister Carney's recent articulation of a sovereignty doctrine for Canada — one that acknowledges what he calls "rupture, not transition" — is welcome. It names the problem correctly. But naming a problem and solving it are separated by the hardest distance in governance: implementation.

## The Greengrocer's Dilemma

Václav Havel's greengrocer placed a sign in his window — "Workers of the world, unite!" — not because he believed it, but because the system required the gesture. Havel's point was that complicity with an unjust order begins with small accommodations.

Canada's digital dependency operates on similar logic. We use foreign cloud infrastructure not because we have evaluated it and chosen it, but because it is the default. We route our most sensitive government data through architectures controlled by foreign jurisdictions not by decision, but by inertia.

The sovereignty that matters is not the sovereignty you proclaim. It is the sovereignty that survives contact with reality.

## What Resilience Requires

A sovereign digital posture that can "take a punch" requires three things: redundancy in critical infrastructure, domestic capacity for AI inference, and cryptographic independence. None of these are glamorous. All of them are essential.
